
- [ ] Explain why the presentation, and each part of it, matters.<br />
  [The Power of Explaining the “Why” in Decision-Making | Jay Vilalta's Blog](https://jayvilalta.com/blog/2025/01/13/the-power-of-explaining-why/)
- [ ] Begin with some quick survey questions.<br />
  "Raise your hand if you "x", or similar.<br />
  This gets the audience engaged. At the same time, you get to learn about the audience, which enables you to tailor your presentation better to them.
- [ ] Steady pace of delivery.<br />
  Don’t slow down and then suddenly speed up. Keep delivery steady, so the audience can absorb your content.
- [ ] Be relatable to the audience.<br />
  Show a little vulnerability. Avoid distancing them. Avoid arousing envy or pity.
- [ ] When showing an important slide, allow (even invite) the audience to take snap.<br />
  They might share it on social media, etc. which spreads your material to a wider audience.
- [ ] Use self-deprecating humour.
- [ ] Tell a story with a typical narrative arc.<br />
  Example: [Three-act structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-act_structure) – Setup, Confrontation, Resolution.
- [ ] Use big, legible fonts on slides.
- [ ] Use animation on slides to keep people’s attention.
- [ ] Change your tone of voice occasionally, to keep people’s attention.
- [ ] Don’t read off a script. Speak from memory.
- [ ] Make eye contact with various audience members when speaking in-person.
- [ ] Don’t overuse jokes. Just use a sprinkle of humour.
- [ ] Don’t spend a long time introducing yourself.<br />
  Start offering value as early as possible or even immediately.
- [ ] Be reflexive - incorporate ideas you are presenting into the presentation itself.
- [ ] Provide text-only and audio-only versions of the presentation.
- [ ] Introduce any new or unusual terms or keywords very articulately.<br />
  Say the words *slowly* and repeat twice or more.
- [ ] Use Aristotle's modes of persuasion.<br />
  Ethos: appear credible in your appearance, reputation, and ability to give the speech.<br />
  Pathos: appeal to the audience's emotions. Metaphor, simile, a passionate delivery, or even a simple claim that a matter is unjust.<br />
  Logos: logical appeal or the simulation of it. Describe facts and figures that support the claims.<br />
  [Wiki: Modes of persuasion - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modes_of_persuasion)
